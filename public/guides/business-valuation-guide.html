What This Tool Does

This tool helps you estimate the fair market value of a business using the Capitalisation of Future Maintainable Earnings (CFME) method — the most commonly used valuation approach for Australian small and medium businesses.

In plain English: it looks at how much profit the business makes, adjusts for unusual items, and multiplies by a factor that reflects how risky the business is. It then adds or subtracts balance sheet items to arrive at the total value of the shares.

Who is this for?

  • Business owners wanting to understand what their business is worth before selling, buying, or planning succession
  • Accountants preparing draft valuation reports for clients — the tool does 80% of the analytical work and produces a structured output you can refine
  • Advisers needing a quick but defensible estimate for family law, insurance, or strategic planning purposes

💡 Before you start

Gather the business's financial statements (Profit & Loss and Balance Sheet) for at least the last 2 years, preferably 3. The accountant's annual report as a single PDF is ideal — the tool will extract everything it needs.

📚 Learn more: What is a business valuation?

A business valuation estimates what a willing buyer would pay a willing seller for a business, where neither is under pressure to transact. It's needed for:

  • Selling or buying — sets the starting point for negotiations
  • Succession planning — determines the value for transferring to the next generation
  • Family law — courts require valuations when dividing matrimonial assets
  • Tax purposes — CGT events, share issues, and reorganisations need documented market values
  • Insurance — ensuring adequate cover for business interruption or key person loss

In Australia, formal valuations are governed by APES 225 Valuation Services, issued by the Accounting Professional and Ethical Standards Board. This tool aligns with APES 225 principles but produces a "calculation engagement" — meaning you've agreed the method and the tool applies it. For a formal "valuation engagement," an independent professional valuer should review and sign off on the output.

1 Engagement Setup

This step collects basic information about the business and your valuation parameters. Everything entered here flows into the final report.

Required Fields

  • Business Name — the legal entity name (e.g., "Smith & Co Pty Ltd")
  • Valuation Date — the effective date of the valuation, usually the most recent financial year end (e.g., "30/06/2024"). Type it in any format.
  • Industry Sector — select the closest match. This feeds the AI's industry analysis and helps determine appropriate multiples.
  • Business Description — write at least a paragraph describing what the business does, its key products/services, competitive position, and markets. The AI uses this extensively — more detail produces a better report.

Valuation Method: EBITDA vs SDE

You must choose between two methods. This is an important decision:

MethodBest ForHow It Works
EBITDA Businesses being sold to a company, competitor, or investor who will hire management Values the business operations. The owner's salary stays as a cost because a replacement manager will be needed.
SDE Small businesses being sold to an owner-operator who will run the business themselves Adds the owner's total compensation back to EBITDA. The buyer "pays themselves" from the SDE and the remainder services the purchase price.
📝 Example: Choosing the right method

A plumbing business with 8 employees makes $200k EBITDA. The owner takes $150k salary. If sold to a competitor (who has their own managers), use EBITDA ($200k). If sold to a plumber who'll run it themselves, use SDE ($200k + $150k = $350k).

Valuation Scope: Enterprise vs Equity

  • Enterprise Value Only — just the value of the business operations (suitable for asset sales)
  • Full Entity (Equity) Value — the enterprise value plus/minus balance sheet items, giving you the value of the shares (required for share sales)
📚 Learn more: Enterprise Value vs Equity Value

Enterprise Value (EV) is the value of the business as a money-making machine. It answers: "How much is the earning capacity worth?"

Equity Value answers: "How much are the shares worth?" It takes the EV and adjusts for everything else the company owns and owes:

Equity Value = Enterprise Value + Cash + Debtors + Stock − Creditors − Provisions − Tax Liabilities + Surplus Assets − Debt

Think of it this way: if you buy all the shares, you get everything — the earning capacity AND all the cash in the bank, the money owed by customers, the stock on the shelves, but also the bills to pay and the employee leave owing.

2 Upload Financial Data

Upload the business's financial statements. The AI will read the PDF and extract every line item into a structured format you can review and edit.

What to Upload

  • Financial Statements (required) — the accountant's annual report, typically a single PDF containing the Profit & Loss, Balance Sheet, Notes, and Depreciation Schedule. Upload reports for 2-4 financial years.
  • General Ledger (optional) — if the bookkeeper can export the GL as Excel or CSV, upload it for more accurate normalisation analysis. The GL shows individual transactions within each expense category.
💡 Best practice

Upload the statutory accounts prepared by the external accountant — these are the "source of truth" that banks, the ATO, and courts will accept. Management accounts or Xero/MYOB reports can differ due to year-end adjustments.

After Uploading

  1. Click "Parse with AI" and wait 30-60 seconds
  2. The AI extracts every P&L line item and balance sheet item, categorised by type
  3. Review the data in the spreadsheet grid — correct any errors
  4. Check that Depreciation is categorised as "Depreciation" (not "Operating Expenses")
  5. Check that Interest is categorised as "Interest" (not "Operating Expenses")
  6. Check that Tax is categorised as "Tax" if present
⚠️ Common issue: Garbled PDF text

Some older PDFs use embedded fonts that can't be extracted as text. If you see garbled characters (e.g., ")LQDQFLDO") for one year but not others, that year's PDF has this issue. You'll need to enter that year's data manually using the "+ add" buttons, or ask the accountant for a newer version of the report.

3 EBITDA Calculation

This step shows the EBITDA build-up for each year. EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation and Amortisation.

What to Check

  • Does the Revenue match the financial statements?
  • Does the Net Profit match? If not, a line item may be miscategorised — go back to Step 2
  • Are the add-backs correct? Depreciation, amortisation, interest, and tax should all show as green numbers being added back
📝 Example: EBITDA calculation
Revenue$10,000,000
Less: Cost of Sales($3,600,000)
Gross Profit$6,400,000
Less: Operating Expenses($5,800,000)
Less: Depreciation($88,000)
Less: Interest($15,000)
Less: Tax($162,000)
Net Profit$335,000
EBITDA Build-up:
Net Profit$335,000
Add: Depreciation$88,000
Add: Interest$15,000
Add: Tax$162,000
EBITDA$600,000

Click "Confirm & Continue" once you're satisfied the numbers are correct.

📚 Learn more: Why use EBITDA instead of Net Profit?

Net profit is affected by how the business is financed (interest), the tax structure, and accounting policies (depreciation methods). These factors vary between businesses and between buyers. EBITDA strips them out to show the operating earning power of the business — what it generates before financing and accounting decisions.

This makes EBITDA the best basis for comparing businesses and for applying an earnings multiple.

4 Normalisation Adjustments

This is the most important step in the valuation. Normalisation adjusts the historical EBITDA to show what a new owner could reasonably expect to earn. Every dollar of adjustment, multiplied by the earnings multiple, directly changes the business value.

Why Normalise?

Business owners routinely run personal expenses through the business to reduce tax. They may pay themselves above or below market rate. There may be one-off events that inflated or depressed profits. A buyer needs to see the true, sustainable earning power — not the tax-minimised version.

How to Use This Step

  1. Click "🤖 AI Analysis" — the AI scans every operating expense and flags potential adjustments with reasoning
  2. Review each flagged item. For each one, choose:
    • Accept — you agree with the AI's recommendation
    • Reject — you disagree; the item is kept but recorded as "considered and rejected" (this is good practice)
    • Modify — you agree it's an adjustment but want to change the amount
  3. Add any items the AI missed using the manual buttons
  4. Review the Normalised EBITDA summary at the bottom

The Five Categories of Normalisation

1. Owner Compensation

The owner's salary, superannuation, and benefits may not reflect market rates. If the owner takes $250,000 but a replacement manager would cost $150,000, we add back $100,000. If the owner takes only $80,000, we deduct $70,000 to bring it to market rate.

What to look for: Wages line items with owner's name, excessive superannuation contributions, personal insurance policies paid by the business.

2. Related Party Transactions

Transactions with entities related to the owner — rent paid to the owner's property trust, management fees to the owner's holding company, wages to family members who aren't essential to operations.

What to look for: Management fees, rent to associated entities, wages to family members (check if they're doing real work at market rates).

3. Personal / Discretionary Expenses

Expenses that benefit the owner personally rather than the business. These cease when the business is sold.

What to look for: Travel & accommodation (personal holidays coded as business trips), meals & entertainment (personal dining), motor vehicle expenses (personal use component), donations & sponsorships (owner's pet causes), club memberships, personal mobile phones.

4. Non-Recurring Items

One-off events that won't happen again. These distort the picture of normal ongoing profitability.

What to look for: One-off legal fees (lawsuit settlement), restructuring costs, insurance claims received, government grants (JobKeeper, COVID subsidies), large bad debt write-offs, profit/loss on asset sales.

5. Non-Operating Items

Income or expenses not related to the core business operations. These are valued separately (usually as surplus assets on the balance sheet).

What to look for: Interest income (comes from cash holdings, valued as surplus asset), rental income from non-core property, investment income, foreign exchange gains/losses.

📝 Worked Example: Normalisation

A construction business has EBITDA of $600,000. Analysis reveals:

ItemCategoryTreatmentAmount
Owner salary $300k vs market $180kOwner CompAdd back excess+$120,000
Management fee to owner's trustRelated PartyRemove (no real service)+$80,000
Owner's personal travelPersonalAdd back+$25,000
Meals & entertainmentPersonalAdd back 50%+$7,000
COVID government grantNon-RecurringRemove (won't recur)−$50,000
Interest income on cashNon-OperatingRemove (valued in BS)−$19,000

At a 3x multiple, these adjustments changed the enterprise value from $1,800,000 to $2,289,000 — a difference of $489,000.

⚠️ Be conservative with add-backs

Aggressive normalisation adjustments erode buyer confidence. Every add-back will be scrutinised during due diligence. If you can't clearly justify an adjustment, don't make it. The AI recommends "Leave as-is" for items that are borderline — trust that judgment.

📚 Learn more: Normalisation best practice

Documentation is everything. For every adjustment, ask: "Could I explain this to a sceptical buyer with evidence?" If yes, include it. If no, leave it out.

The hidden deduction: If the owner takes $50,000 salary but the market rate for their role is $150,000, the normalised EBITDA decreases by $100,000. Many sellers forget this works both ways. At a 3x multiple, this represents $300,000 off the business value.

Consistency across years: If you add back travel in 2024, add it back in 2023 and 2022 too. Inconsistent adjustments suggest cherry-picking.

Items the AI commonly flags:

  • Management fees to associated entities (very common in family groups)
  • Motor vehicle expenses (especially if the owner drives a prestige vehicle)
  • Travel that seems disproportionate to the business's needs
  • Donations and sponsorships
  • Insurance premiums that include personal policies
  • Labour hire or subcontractor costs to related parties

5 Weighting & Future Maintainable Earnings

Now that you have the normalised EBITDA for each year, you need to derive a single number — the Future Maintainable Earnings (FME). This is the amount you multiply by the EBITDA multiple to get the enterprise value.

Why Not Just Use the Latest Year?

Because any single year might be unusually good or bad. By weighting across multiple years, you smooth out anomalies and get a more reliable picture of what the business can sustainably earn going forward.

How Weighting Works

Each year gets a percentage weight (must sum to 100%). The FME is the weighted average:

FME = (Year 1 EBITDA × Weight%) + (Year 2 EBITDA × Weight%) + (Year 3 EBITDA × Weight%)

General Principles

  • More recent years get higher weight — they better reflect current performance and market conditions
  • Default weighting is proportional to recency (e.g., 50% / 33% / 17% for 3 years)
  • Unusual years get less weight — but you must explain why in the context box
  • The AI will recommend weights based on the pattern it sees, but you can override
📝 Worked Example: Weighting with an unusual year

A business has normalised EBITDA of:

YearEBITDAContext
FY2024$700,000Normal year, new contract won
FY2023$300,000Lost major customer mid-year (since replaced)
FY2022$650,000Normal year

Without context, a simple average gives FME of $550,000. But FY2023 was an anomaly — the lost customer has been replaced. The AI might recommend:

YearEBITDAWeightContribution
FY2024$700,00060%$420,000
FY2023$300,00010%$30,000
FY2022$650,00030%$195,000

The user's explanation ("Lost major customer in FY23 who has since been replaced with three smaller customers") is included in the valuation report, providing the reasoning trail a reviewer needs.

Using the AI Weighting Analysis

  1. Enter any context about unusual years in the text box
  2. Click "🤖 AI Weighting Analysis"
  3. The AI considers the EBITDA pattern AND your context, then recommends weights with reasoning
  4. Review and adjust using the sliders or number inputs
  5. Weights must sum to 100% (shown in green when correct, red when not)
  6. The FME is displayed in the large navy box at the bottom
💡 Pro tip: Context matters enormously

A year with low EBITDA could mean the business is declining (bad sign — increase its weight) OR that something unusual happened (reduce its weight). The AI can't tell the difference without your context. Always explain anomalies — it directly affects the FME and therefore the final value.

📚 Learn more: Common weighting scenarios

Steadily growing business: Weight more heavily to recent years (e.g., 55/30/15). The recent performance is more representative of future earnings.

Business recovering from COVID: If FY2021 was badly affected, reduce its weight significantly (e.g., 60/30/10) and explain the COVID impact in the context box.

Cyclical business: If the business follows economic cycles, include a full cycle in the weighting (e.g., 3-4 years) with more even weights to average out the peaks and troughs.

Business with one exceptional year: If one year had a non-recurring windfall (e.g., a one-off large contract), reduce its weight. But first check — should this have been handled in normalisation instead?

Declining business: If EBITDA is trending downward and there's no good reason to expect a reversal, weight heavily to the most recent year. This is a difficult conversation with the seller but produces an honest valuation.

6 Risk Analysis & EBITDA Multiple

The EBITDA multiple reflects how risky the business is. A safer, more predictable business commands a higher multiple (higher price). A riskier business gets a lower multiple.

The Six Risk Factors

Each factor is scored from 0 (very low risk) to 10 (very high risk), with both an optimistic (low) and conservative (high) score to create a range.

FactorWhat to Consider
Revenue GrowthIs revenue growing consistently? Flat? Declining? A growing business with sticky revenue is low risk. A business dependent on winning new work each year is higher risk.
Financial RiskHow much debt does the business carry? Is liquidity strong? A debt-free business with cash reserves is very low risk.
Key PersonWould the business survive if the owner left? If it relies heavily on one person's relationships or skills, that's high risk.
Customer ConcentrationDoes one customer account for more than 20% of revenue? If one customer leaving would cripple the business, that's high risk.
ProfitabilityAre margins consistent and healthy? A 20%+ EBITDA margin is strong. Volatile or thin margins indicate higher risk.
CompetitionHow competitive is the market? Are there barriers to entry? A regulated monopoly is very low risk. A crowded market with no switching costs is high risk.

How Scores Map to Multiples

Composite ScoreMultipleDescription
2-3 out of 10~5xVery safe, predictable business
5 out of 10~3xAverage risk
8-9 out of 10~1xVery high risk

For Australian SMEs, typical multiples range from 1x to 6x EBITDA. Most small businesses sell at 2-4x.

7 Balance Sheet Review

This step only appears if you selected "Full Entity (Equity) Value" in Step 1. You're now classifying every balance sheet item to determine what adds to or subtracts from the enterprise value.

The Five Classifications

ClassificationWhat Goes HereEffect on Value
🔧 In Enterprise ValueOperating fixed assets: plant, equipment, vehicles, fit-outNo separate effect — already priced into the multiple
➕ Transfers (Asset)Cash, trade debtors, stock, prepayments, tax assetsAdded to enterprise value
➖ Transfers (Liability)Trade creditors, employee provisions, tax liabilities, GSTDeducted from enterprise value
⭐ SurplusExcess cash, investments, director loans, non-operating propertyAdded to enterprise value
🏦 DebtBank loans, hire purchase, finance leasesDeducted from enterprise value
⚠️ Key principle: Don't double-count operating fixed assets

Plant, equipment, and vehicles used in operations are already valued WITHIN the enterprise value. The EBITDA multiple prices them in — the buyer can't earn the EBITDA without them. Classify these as "🔧 In Enterprise Value" and they will NOT be added or subtracted.

Adjusting Book Values

The "Adjusted Value" column lets you override book values with estimated market values:

  • Trade debtors: Reduce for any doubtful debts not already provided for
  • Stock: Reduce for any obsolete or slow-moving stock
  • Plant & equipment: If book value differs materially from market/replacement value
  • Employee provisions: Ensure they're adequate for actual entitlements

📚 Learn more: Why transferring items matter

When you buy all the shares of a company, you get everything the company owns and owes. The enterprise value only prices the earning capacity. But the buyer also receives:

  • The cash sitting in the bank accounts
  • The money owed by customers (debtors) — real money that will be collected
  • The stock on the shelves — that has real value

But the buyer also inherits:

  • The bills to pay (creditors)
  • The employee leave owing (annual leave, long service leave)
  • Any tax the company owes

The net of all these transferring items can be a very significant number — often $1M+ for a $10M revenue business. Getting this right is critical to an accurate equity value.

8 Valuation Summary

This step shows the complete valuation calculation. No new inputs are needed — review the numbers and check they make sense.

What to Check

  • Does the enterprise value range feel right for a business of this size and type?
  • Is the balance sheet adjustment reasonable? (A very large positive or negative number may indicate a classification error in Step 7)
  • Does the implied revenue multiple make sense for the industry?
  • Is the equipment floor value warning triggered? If so, consider whether the going concern assumption is appropriate
  • Check the sensitivity matrix — does the range of outcomes feel reasonable?

Discounts

Consider whether discounts should apply:

  • Minority discount: Only if valuing less than 100% of the shares
  • Marketability discount: If the shares are in a private company with no ready market. Not always appropriate — depends on the purpose of the valuation.
📚 Learn more: When to apply discounts

Minority discount (15-30%): A 20% shareholder has much less control than a 100% owner. They can't set dividends, hire/fire staff, or sell the business. Their shares are worth proportionally less. Apply only when valuing a minority interest.

Marketability discount (10-25%): Private company shares can't be sold on a stock exchange. There's no ready market. This reduces their value compared to equivalent listed shares. Common in family law and estate planning valuations.

When NOT to discount: If the purpose is a full sale to a single buyer, no discounts are needed — the buyer is getting 100% control and will create their own liquidity by owning the whole business.
